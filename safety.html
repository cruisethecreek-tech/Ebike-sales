The basics

Riding an e-bike

Riding an e-bike is a wonderful experience opening up the joy of cycling to a wider audience than ever before. For many, riding an e-bike will be the first time they have ridden a bike since they were young — and in many ways riding an e-bike is just like riding a standard bike. Pedaling is usually involved (unless you are using an e-bike with a throttle-only option), and it involves forward motion and balance to stay upright just like a standard bike. Depending upon the model, it also can involve shifting the rear cluster of gears to maximize your effort according to the terrain you're riding.

How they're different

Bike vs. e-bike

Although there are some similarities, there are also some significant differences between bikes and e-bikes. Whereas a competition road bike can weigh less than 20 lbs., a typical e-bike weighs anywhere from 45 to 65 lbs. depending upon the size and style of the bike. Additionally, depending upon the Class of e-bike you're riding, an e-bike can go up to and sustain 20 mph on a Class II e-bike and up to 28 mph on most Class III e-bikes (some select models can go up to 36 mph, which we do not carry) — whereas sustaining or reaching the same speeds on a standard bike only occurs when you are "topped out" or are going downhill.

Our bikes will not exceed 28 mph on flat land.

As this is the case, it is always important to keep foremost in your mind how to safely operate your e-bike. Below are the important items to be aware of when operating one.

Before you ride

Gear & protection

Helmets

First and foremost, always wear a bike helmet — properly fitted snugly so it doesn't move around on your head. As part of any rental, Cruise the Creek provides a properly fitted helmet. Helmets are optional but always recommended.

Eye Protection

Wear prescription glasses if you need them, or a pair of sport glasses designed for cycling. It is not uncommon to be hit with flying insects, especially in open country, and at e-bike speeds an impact can damage your eyes. We provide properly fitted eyewear with every rental.

Skin Exposure

If you plan on being out for several hours, use a good sunscreen (SPF 30 or higher depending on your skin type) — unless you're wearing clothing that covers your arms, which are most prone to burning while riding.

Cycling Apparel

It is not necessary to ride in lycra. But if you haven't been on a bike in a while, consider riding shorts with a padded seat (sewn-in or insert). Most of our bikes use gel-padded saddles that are adequate for short rides; for longer rides, padded shorts help. We also stock e-bikes retrofitted with a suspension seat post — a shock absorber that softens the ride if you'd rather skip the padded shorts.

On the road

Rules of the road

Always follow the rules of the road when operating an e-bike. For now, when riding Class II and III e-bikes, you are required to follow the same rules of the road that standard bikes follow as described in the Vehicle Code under Bicyclist and Pedestrian Safety. Always remember that in a collision with a vehicle, the cyclist is at a significant disadvantage due to the immense difference in mass — so remain vigilant of traffic and always presume the vehicle has the right of way until you have confirmed the driver sees you and has yielded.

Note: due to higher speed capacity, additional vehicle codes are being developed to govern Class III e-bikes. In the near future, Class III e-bikes may require licensing similar to registering a car or motorcycle.

Always ride in the bike lane when available, or as close to the right-hand shoulder as safely possible when no lane is available. Ride with the flow of traffic, pay attention to all signs and signals, ride single file, and presume that a vehicle does not see you before proceeding.

Fit matters

Sizing

Make sure you've been properly fitted on the right size bike frame, with the appropriate seat and handlebar height and angle. Almost all e-bikes come in three sizes — Small, Medium, Large — chosen mainly by the rider's inseam and ability to stabilize the bike at a standstill with feet touching the ground.

There are two styles of frames: a step-through frame with no top tube (you can step through it when mounting), and a standard frame with a top tube you have to step over. Pick the style that's most comfortable for you.

Mechanics

Shifting

Like most standard bikes, many e-bikes have seven gears on the rear hub, selected by a trigger shifter on the right side of the handlebar. One trigger upshifts to a lower (easier) gear — the larger chainrings used for climbing — and another trigger shifts to a higher (harder) gear — the smaller chainrings used for higher speeds on flat terrain. There is only one front chainring, so no front shifting is needed. Single-speed e-bikes don't shift at all.

You can throw a chain on any bike, but more care is needed when shifting an e-bike because of the extra torque from the motor. Shift one gear at a time with a slight hesitation in your pedaling, and confirm the gear has changed before shifting again. If you click the trigger several times without easing off, the chain can "jump" the front chainring — at which point you'll need to grab the chain, place it on top of the chainring, and turn the chainring clockwise to guide it back on.

The cockpit

E-bike controller

Every e-bike has a controller mounted on the left side of the handlebar with an on/off button and + / – buttons to select power assist. Most e-bikes have five levels: Eco, Tour, Moderate, Sport & Turbo. Eco is the lowest assist; Tour, Moderate, Sport, and Turbo each step up. The controller also shows total miles traveled, top speed, average speed, accumulated miles, and a battery indicator.

Range & power

Battery & motor

All e-bikes have a rechargeable lithium battery powering the electric motor. Range is hard to specify precisely because of the many variables involved, but most e-bikes have 28 to 55 miles as the maximum range.

Hitting the high end of that range (45–55 miles) requires conserving battery — riding in Eco or Tour at moderate speeds. Ride aggressively in Sport or Turbo and the range drops to roughly 25 to 30 miles per cycle. A typical battery takes about 4 to 6 hours to fully recharge, less if it has a partial charge left.

Plan your ride
Eco & Tour go the distance

If you want maximum range, stay in lower assist modes and pedal at moderate speeds. Save Sport and Turbo for hills and headwinds.

Stopping power

Braking

All e-bikes are fitted with front and rear disc brakes, operated by levers on the handlebars — left side for the front brake, right side for the rear. Squeeze the lever to apply the brake. Apply the rear brake before the front to maintain stability while braking.

Closing thoughts
